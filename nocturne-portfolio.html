Project Overview

Designing a legacy

Nocturne was born from one observation: gamers spend thousands of hours building emotional connections with games, but existing platforms reduce those experiences to raw statistics.

The Vision

What if your game library felt less like a spreadsheet and more like walking through a personal museum? Each game a leather-bound artifact. Each achievement a framed exhibit. Each memory preserved on aged parchment.

Nocturne replaces the utilitarian with the ceremonial giving gamers a space that honors the emotional weight of their journey.

The Problem

Gaming libraries feel empty

Players own hundreds of games but have no way to express why any of them matter. Achievements sit in endless lists. Collections have no soul. The emotional story gets lost.

PAIN POINT 01

"I have 200+ games but can't tell you which ones shaped me."

Current platforms show playtime and trophies but never the personal story. Life-changing games look the same as five-minute trials.

Source: Top complaint across gaming library app store reviews and Reddit threads

PAIN POINT 02

"My achievements feel hollow without any context."

A rare trophy you spent 40 hours earning sits next to one you got by accident. No way to highlight or explain what matters.

Source: Common theme across gaming community forums and achievement-tracker reviews

PAIN POINT 03

"I want to share my gaming story, not just my stats."

Social features are competitive leaderboards, rankings, KD ratios. No space for storytelling or genuine connection.

PAIN POINT 04

"Gaming shaped my life, but it feels disposable."

Games disappear from storefronts, servers shut down, progress resets. No permanent record of your journey as a player.

The Solution

Your games deserve a museum

Nocturne reimagines your game library as a personal museum. Instead of flat lists wooden shelves, leather spines, and handwritten collector's notes. Every game becomes an artifact worth preserving.

User Journey

From import to exhibition

A five-step path that takes players from connecting their accounts to showcasing a curated public exhibition of their gaming legacy.

1

Connect & Import

Link your Steam, PlayStation, or Xbox account. Games populate automatically onto Archive shelves with playtime, achievements, and purchase dates.

2

Explore Artifacts

Click any game spine on the shelf. Its artifact page opens box art in an ornate frame, "Hours Cherished" playtime, and space for collector's notes.

3

Write Your Story

Open the Chronicle and write a journal entry about a meaningful moment. Entries link to games, auto-timestamp, and support screenshots or clips.

4

Curate Your Gallery

Pin your rarest achievements to the Gallery. Each piece gets an ornate frame, a rarity percentage as museum provenance, and your annotation.

5

Share Your Legacy

Your public profile becomes a museum exhibition. Other collectors browse your shelves, read your chronicle, and discover games through your lens.

Testing Methodology
I evaluated the prototype against Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics and walked through 5 core tasks: (1) Find a specific game in the Archive and read its collector's notes, (2) Write a Chronicle journal entry linked to a game, (3) Curate 3 achievements into a Gallery display, (4) Find another collector with similar taste, (5) Navigate from Archive to Chronicle to Gallery without using search. The "< 12s" find time is a projected estimate based on information architecture depth and comparable app benchmarks.
Reflections

What I'd do differently

Honest trade-offs and the changes I'd make with more time, a team, or actual game data.

REFLECTION 01

"The aesthetic might intimidate casual gamers"

Nocturne's premium feel (gold, parchment, serif fonts) is intentional for the core audience — but it risks alienating more casual players. With more time, I'd design a lighter, more approachable theme as an alternative skin and let users choose.

REFLECTION 02

"Mobile was deprioritized"

Nocturne is desktop-first because gaming libraries are browsed on the same device you play on. But mobile browsing during commutes is a real use case worth addressing. The current responsive scaling works but doesn't feel native — I'd invest in a dedicated mobile architecture.

REFLECTION 03

"The Chronicle needs onboarding"

The journal feature is powerful but the blank canvas can be intimidating without a starting point. With more time, I'd add guided prompts: "What moment from this game will you remember in 10 years?" — giving users direction without constraining creativity.

REFLECTION 04

"Real game data changes everything"

All content is fictional real Steam/PlayStation data would surface edge cases I haven't designed for: games with no cover art, achievements with broken images, libraries with 1000+ games that need virtual scrolling. The shelf metaphor may not scale beyond ~300 games without pagination.
