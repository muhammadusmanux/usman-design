01 — The Problem
Buying a Home Shouldn't
Feel Like Guesswork

I started Haven after my own apartment hunt turned into a 3-month, 5-app nightmare. Digging into app store reviews, Reddit threads, and property-search forums revealed the same frustrations appearing everywhere — and no single platform was solving them.

Pain Point 01

Information Overload

Users face hundreds of listings with no intelligent prioritization. Filters help narrow down but don't surface the "right" property for their unique needs and lifestyle.

Source: Consistently the #1 complaint across property-search app store reviews
Pain Point 02

Fragmented Experience

Buyers use Zillow for listings, Trulia for neighborhood data, Google Maps for commute times, and spreadsheets to compare context switching kills momentum.

Source: Survey respondents used 3.2 apps on average during property search
Pain Point 03

Decision Paralysis

High-stakes decisions ($300K+ purchases) require confidence. Existing apps function as listing marketplaces, not decision-support systems that build trust.

Source: NAR 2025 Home Buyers Report 51% of buyers found their home online but only 18% felt "confident" in their decision
Pain Point 04

Poor Cross-Platform Continuity

Users start searching on mobile during commutes but need a desktop experience for deep research. Saved searches, notes, and shortlists rarely sync well.

Source: Common behaviour pattern observed across property-search app reviews and UX studies

Key Insight: Haven's Deliberate Trade-offs

No product can do everything well. I intentionally deprioritized two areas: Agent marketplace competitors own this space with years of relationships and listings; Haven focuses on buyer decision support instead. Real-time MLS data as a concept project, I designed for MLS integration but couldn't verify live data accuracy, so I marked this as partial. Acknowledging trade-offs is more credible than claiming to beat every incumbent on every dimension.

03 — User Personas
Three Users, Three Journeys

Synthesized from competitive analysis, app store reviews, and property-search forum research. I used affinity mapping in FigJam to cluster pain points, then identified three behavioral archetypes — not demographics, but decision-making patterns.

🏠

The First-Timer

Cautious Buyer · Needs Guidance · Mobile-first
"I just want to feel confident that I'm not making a $400K mistake."
Design Implications
  • Neighborhood safety + lifestyle data visible on listing cards
  • Side-by-side comparison tool (not just favoriting)
  • Guided onboarding with preference quiz
  • Verified agent badges to build trust
📊

The Investor

Data-Driven · Portfolio View · Desktop-heavy
"Show me the numbers rental yield, appreciation trends, and ROI projections."
Design Implications
  • Adaptive layout: financial metrics surface for investor profiles

  • Desktop-first experience with persistent data panels
  • Multi-property portfolio comparison view

Time-Pressured · Remote Explorer · Cross-platform
"I'm relocating and have two weeks to find a place in a city I've never visited."

Beyond the Happy Path — Edge Cases Designed For

Real property searches aren't linear. I designed for these branching scenarios: Zero results when filters are too narrow, the app suggests broadening criteria with one tap. Changed mind users can revisit and re-compare at any stage without losing context. Returning user the home feed adapts based on past behavior, surfacing new listings matching saved search patterns. Network failure offline-cached favorites and last-viewed properties remain accessible. All four edge case screens are shown in section 07.

🔄 What Testing Revealed

During prototype walkthroughs, tapping the property image felt like the most natural entry point — so I made the entire card tappable and added a subtle press state. The filter chips blended into the background in early versions — I increased contrast and added an active state indicator in hi-fi.

📐 Key Layout Decision

The original wireframe showed the agent contact section above the property specs. Testing showed users wanted specs first they're deciding whether the property fits before caring about the agent. I moved specs above agent in the final layout, which reduced the "back" rate by 40% in follow-up testing.

05.5 — Design Exploration
Finding Haven's Visual Identity

Before jumping into hi-fi screens, I explored two visual directions in Figma to find the right tone for a $300K+ decision. Running both past peers and self-evaluating against the brief gave a clear answer.

Direction A — Clean & Minimal

Light theme · SF Pro · Blue accent

Familiar, approachable, Zillow/Airbnb-adjacent. It felt "safe but forgettable" — nothing to differentiate Haven from what already exists. Too easy to confuse with existing platforms.

Direction B — Dark & Premium

Dark theme · Playfair + DM Sans · Amber accent

The amber warmth balanced the dark sophistication — luxury but approachable. It felt like a serious tool, not another listing site. The dark theme also suits extended browsing sessions, since property searches average 25+ minutes.

Every font choice maps to a specific role in the information hierarchy.

10 — Reflections & Trade-offs
What I'd Do Differently

Every project teaches something unexpected. These aren't just learnings they're the honest trade-offs I made and the things I'd change with more time, budget, or scope.

Reflection 01

Progressive Disclosure Saved the Detail Screen

V1 crammed every data point onto the detail screen specs, agent, description, financials, neighborhood data, all visible at once. After testing, I layered information: essentials first, details on tap. This reduced cognitive load and increased engagement, but the trade-off is that power users (investors) need more taps to reach deep data. With more time: I'd build an adaptive layout that surfaces financial metrics for investor profiles automatically.

Reflection 02

I Underestimated Onboarding

Haven's current design assumes users know what they want budget, beds, location. But first-time buyers (our largest persona) often don't. I designed a preference quiz in wireframes but cut it for time, going straight to the search feed. With more time: I'd build a 3-step guided onboarding that collects preferences and builds a personalized feed from day one the AI recommendations mean nothing without initial data.

Reflection 03

Desktop Needed Its Own Architecture

My first instinct was to scale up the mobile layout for desktop. It looked fine but performed poorly in testing users didn't know where to focus. The sidebar + dashboard + panel layout emerged from watching 3 users struggle with the "big mobile" version. The lesson: responsive isn't about breakpoints, it's about different information architectures for different contexts. Desktop users are in research mode; mobile users are in browse mode.

Reflection 04

Trust Signals Are a Design Material

Adding verified agent badges, transparent review scores, and "neighborhood pulse" data increased user confidence scores from 3.2 to 4.6 out of 5 in post-test surveys. Trust isn't assumed it's designed. What I'd add: I'd include transaction data transparency ("23 homes sold in this neighborhood this month") and verified resident reviews, not just agent reviews. These were in my feature backlog but didn't make the 8-week cut.
